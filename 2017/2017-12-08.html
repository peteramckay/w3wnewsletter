Recent independence-related info from other sites around the web:

  • The Bloomberg Surveillance business podcast has named Scott Galloway's book The Four, which covers the increasing economic dominance of Amazon, Apple, Alphabet, and Facebook, as its Book of the Year. Having recently read this one myself, I can vouch it's a well-deserved honor for Galloway. Congrats!
  • The nonprofit Freedom House recently released its annual Freedom on the Net report for 2017. It didn't paint a pretty picture at all, with a heavy focus this year on the use of disinformation on social media. The U.S., which has infamously struggled with that issue of late, was one of 18 countries who have seen such futzing with their elections over the past year. Freedom House also said in general that internet freedom around the world fell for a seventh straight year in 2017. And, for the third year in a row, China was the world's single biggest abuser of internet freedom.
  • The Chicago Tribune recently (and somewhat surprisingly, for a media company) editorialized in favor of the Federal Communications Commission's pending proposal to end net neutrality. While I still heartily disagree with this idea myself, I'm sharing the link in the spirit of open debate and because I'm confident the Trib's opinion is sincerely held, at least. That's an extreme rarity among pro-FCC arguments circulating on the web lately about this issue, the vast majority of which have pretty clearly been bought and paid for by the telcom industry.
  • ICYMI: Apple has been having serious software problems lately, including an embarrassing security breach on the Mac and a botched rollout of iOS 11, the latest update to its mobile operating system. So, yeah, that pretty much covers every device the company makes. Over at TWiT.tv, security expert Steve Gibson, who's generally a fan of iOS, dubbed its issues in particular "a disaster" on the latest episode of his show Security Now.
  • The New York Times's latest quarterly report to investors showed really promising user growth, driven by digital subscriptions. Business Insider reports: "The New York Times announced it now has 130 million monthly readers and 3.5 million paid subscriptions, which is more than double the company’s subscriber count since Q3 2015."
  • Rock star Neil Young has launched his own website with a decades-long archive of his recordings. In an introductory message, he openly cast the project as a counterstrike against Apple, whose practice of selling lower-quality audio has particularly irritated Young over the years.
  • CNET reports that the venerable text message just passed a milestone anniversary. The first SMS message was sent Dec. 3, 1992, which means the medium is now 25 years old. (A hat tip to developer Ashley Fletcher, aka @fletcher0xFF, for alerting us to this one.)

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr this week. If you have ideas for links or stories we should cover, please do pass them along via either Twitter or email.
