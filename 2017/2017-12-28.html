Recent independence-related info from other sites around the web:

  • Year-in-review thinkpieces are popping up on various tech sites as 2017 winds down. A big theme recurring is that the general public's honeymoon with Silicon Valley basically ended this year. That of course naturally leads to questions about what comes next. Of the posts in this little genre that I've read so far, VC Hermant Taneja's piece for TechCrunch strikes me as probably the best of the bunch. Taneja is frank about the pitfalls the tech industry stumbled into in '17, but he's also optimistic about a potential shift to more "sustainable innovation" in the New Year.
  • ICYMI: DuckDuckGo published a comprehensive guide this fall about how to live without Google. The post covers not just search, where DuckDuckGo competes directly with Google, but also the larger company's secondary products like Gmail, Drive, YouTube, et cetera.
  • Writing for the American Civil Liberties Union's website, Michael Macleod-Ball details how net neutrality can yet be saved in the U.S.
  • CNN recently asked 26 veteran journalists, educators, politicians, and other contributors for their thoughts on why a free press matters. Much food for thought among the responses.

  • The Freedom of the Press Foundation, led by Edward Snowden, has released a new "personal security" app for iOS and Android, called Haven. The app basically turns a "burner" phone into a countersurveillance device by using its mobile senors to monitor a hotel room or other environment while you're away. Thus you can know if anyone breaks into the room and tampers with other devices, plants a bug, et cetera.

    I have to say, the use case for this one seems limited to me offhand. I can see why a cause celebre dissident like Snowden, who might plausibly be trailed personally by swarthy Bond villain types in his daily life, might need something like this. But for the general population, the biggest mobile security problem tends to be too many damn sensors collecting too much damn data on us all the damn time, and the potential that data might be appropriated and abused. Usually, this is done through remote and/or batched attacks that collect data in bulk over the internet, as Snowden himself described in the leaks that made him famous.

    For most people, then, the simple solution should be to turn sensors off and limit permissions on your mobile apps more aggressively. It shouldn't be to fight fire with fire, in my opinion.

  • A good business story by the New York Times recently on the rise of economic "megacities" like New York, San Francisco, and London. These megacities tend to have much more in common with each other, regardless of national borders, than they do with rural areas in their respective "home" countries, the Times reports.
