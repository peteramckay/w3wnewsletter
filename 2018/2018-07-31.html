Re-booting the Indizr newsletter

Howdy! A quick, well overdue update here for subscribers to the Indizr Weekly e-newsletter.

You may have noticed, it's been awhile since you last received that publication. Three months, to be exact. I apologize for the radio silence.

Frankly, my time has been crunched lately due to consulting and freelancing work to pay the bills. Hence the newsletter's abrupt hiatus.

The good news is that I'm new ready to get going again, albeit on a more occasional basis. We won't be back on a regular weekly schedule, but whenever there's something I think is worth reporting to you about tech, on an as-needed basis, I'll send an update.

The editorial approach will change slightly as well, informed by my recent client work here in the San Francisco Bay Area.

The focus of the old Indizr Weekly newsletter was "digital independence," defined broadly. With this new iteration, we'll be focusing on one particular issue under that umbrella -- centralization versus decentralization of the internet.

In short, centralization is running amok online these days. In fact, I'm now convinced it is the defining problem anyone who cares about the internet should now be concerned about.

Some examples of what I mean by centralization:

  • All your social data being stored on one company's servers, like Facebook. We've seen how disastrously this situation can be abused, ultimately for the social provider's profit alone and to the detriment of democracy itself.
  • Any situation in which a business is subject to the phenomenon of "vendor lock-in." This usually entails a tech company exercising control over mission-critical information or functionality for the business lest the tech company gets paid first.
  • Your personal financial information being controlled by large private institutions like Equifax, which was notoriously hacked last year.

From here out, I'm dedicating Indizr to reversing this dynamic for the sake of both individual users and enterprises. The solution, I believe, is "Web 3.0" technology. That's still an evolving buzzword out here in Silicon Valley, but in my opinion, it encompasses anything that works against centralization.

The hottest technology right now that fits that bill -- and the one whose community has most aggressively embraced the term Web 3.0 -- is the blockchain. But I would say other things might still also plausibly be included under the Web 3.0 umbrella at this verrrrrry early stage. Stuff like semantic HTML, which has notably been promoted by worldwide web inventor Sir Tim Berners-Lee via his Solid project at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. I'd say the open Linux operating system is also very Web 3.0 at its heart. And there's other stuff as well.

If you know other folks who might be interested in learning more about decentralization, please encourage them to sign up for the newsletter. The form is here.

If you know a business or organization that's interested in implementing some form of decentralization, that's great too, as I'm aiming to develop a consulting practice in this area. I'd love to hear from leaders of such enterprises anytime via my email peter@indizr.com.
