Recent independence-related info from around the web:

  • The Economist Intelligence Unit just downgraded the U.S. in its widely followed democracy index. It's now a "flawed democracy," down from a "full democracy" previously, according to the magazine's research arm. Among other factors, the Economist cited a long-term decline in popular trust in U.S. government and elected representatives to "extremely low levels." Separately, longtime New York Times columnist Tom Friedman sounded an uncharacteristically urgent warning about the specific threat that Donald Trump poses to American democracy. And the Columbia Journalism Review says that the U.S. is rapidly losing its status as a beacon of press freedom.
  • One year after protracted sit-ins opposing construction of the Dakota Access Pipeline, bills have been introduced in 30 U.S. states to restrict protests in various ways. Although the bills haven't all been enacted, and many likely couldn't withstand constitutional court challenges, they suggest a disturbing political trend, according to the American Civil Liberties Union.
  • Twitter is imposing hard limits on automated user activity, to combat abusive behavior and misinformation. The network's Silicon Valley rivals, including Facebook, have so far stopped short of such restrictions. But Twitter may nevertheless be falling behind in key strategic areas as a company due to indecision and turmoil in the executive suite, according to Vanity Fair.
  • FYI & FWIW: A handy, plain-English primer on "deep learning" by computers, by data scientist Pradeep Menon. (This one was recently recommended on Twitter by Kirk Borne, an astrophysics Ph.D. who's chief data scientist at the consulting giant Booz Allen Hamilton. So I'm confident it's worth passing on :-)
  • Microsoft founder Bill Gates is warning that Silicon Valley's largest companies seem headed for anti-trust trouble in Washington, perhaps akin to his company's legal battles in the late 1990s. Speaking of which...
  • Charles Duhigg reports in the New York Times Sunday Magazine that some of Google's sharp-elbowed business practices may cross the line into anticompetitive territory.
  • A nascent movement of "zebra" tech startups is forming, with aspirations to be more humane, diverse, and community-focused than the typical Silicon Valley startup model has allowed. Since SV refers to its breakout companies as "unicorns," the "zebra" founders picked a different animal to distinguish themselves.
  • Wired reports that Facebook's new virtual private network app, Onavo Protect, leaves a lot to be desired on privacy. Which is, you know, basically the entire point of using a VPN.
  • Intentionally or not, the House that Zuck Built also seems to be at a point of threatening "armageddon" for journalism, writes veteran tech journalist Mathew Ingram in the Columbia Journalism Review.
  • Latvian officials say Russian hackers also appear to be exacerbating an unfolding banking crisis in their country. “There is a high probability that an externally organized widespread information operation is being carried out that, by its structure and execution, is identical to those observed in pre-election periods in the U.S., France and Germany,” Latvia’s Defense Ministry said in an emailed statement to Bloomberg News on Tuesday.
  • The Verge reports that Google's Chrome browser will mark all unencrypted HTTP sites as "not secure" starting in July.
