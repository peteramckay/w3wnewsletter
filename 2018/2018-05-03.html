Big Tech's platforms suck at news. Help us build better ones.

We've started an open-source project, the Digital News Delivery Working Group, to find better ways to get news to people using the web. (We call the group "downdog" for short, like the yoga pose :-) If you'd like to participate, the best way is to join the DNDWG email group by sending a message to dndwg-subscribe@yahoogroups.com. That group is our chief venue for brainstorming and collaborating. Please join us!

Recent news from around the web about news innovation:

  • The #DeleteFacebook movement gained a notable convert of sorts this week: WhatsApp co-founder Jan Koum. Time magazine notes that Koum, whose company was acquired by Facebook in 2014, still has over $1 billion in options that won't vest if he departs the company early. Nevertheless, Koum was adamant in a public Facebook post Monday that he believes now is the time for him to "move on." Multiple news organizations are reporting Koum has clashed with other executives recently about how to address Facebook's privacy crisis. Koum steered clear of mentioning privacy directly in his public statement, however.
  • The New York Times reports that President Xi Jinping is tightening his government's grip on China's major tech companies, including Alibaba and Tencent.
  • Writing for BuzzFeed, Middle East researcher Evan Hill worries that information about major historic events like the Arab Spring may be lost to future generations due to our over-reliance on proprietary tech platforms. Big social networks could bury or even delete valuable eyewitness accounts of history at their whim, Hill says.
  • The Electronic Frontier Foundation published an updated learning module on choosing the right tools to protect your personal data online.
  • Another year, another controversy over whether a comedian's routine at the White House Correspondents Association's annual gala crossed the line. I for one heartily endorse Columbia Journalism Review editor Kyle Pope's recommendation to just end the overly clubby, asking-for-trouble event once and for all. (Disclosure: I worked for Pope for a few years when I was a beat reporter at the Wall Street Journal.)
  • University of Florida journalism professor Jasmine McNealy needed a hug after watching Avengers: Infinity War. We feel your pain, Jasmine.
