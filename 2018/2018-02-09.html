Recent independence-related news from around the web:

  • News organizations are tightening their embrace of digital subscriptions as an answer to the tightening stranglehold that Google and Facebook have on advertising. The New York Times announced that its paywall revenue surpassed $1 billion for the first time in 2017. And Wired announced that it is launching its first paywall. But it remains to be seen whether local and regional organizations -- the rank and file of the U.S. news industry -- can succeed with such a model.
  • Motherboard reports that a new privacy law due to take effect in Europe soon may undermine the maintenance of WHOIS data, an open registry of website publishers that's historically been a bedrock part of the Internet. That means the new law may backfire, enable greater abuses by bad actors on the web, and thus undermine user protections, Motherboard says.
  • YouTube's algorithms sometimes clearly highlight false information over verified stuff, the Guardian reports.
  • A right-wing laywer has filed a slander lawsuit against University of California professor George Lakoff for his comments on an (obviously sketchy and well-publicized) meeting between Trump campaign officials and Russian operatives in Trump Tower during the 2016 presidential campaign. Lakoff has since started a legal defense fund on GoFundMe to protect himself an open-faced attempt to undermine his right to speak.
  • Electronic Frontier Foundation founder Perry Barlow died in his sleep at the age of 70 on Wednesday. His EFF colleague Cindy Cohen wrote in remembrance: "It is no exaggeration to say that major parts of the Internet we all know and love today exist and thrive because of Barlow’s vision and leadership. He always saw the internet as a fundamental place of freedom, where voices long silenced can find an audience and people can connect with others regardless of physical distance.
  • A wiseass on a bicycle recently took to purposefully slowing vehicular traffic outside the Federal Communications Commission's Washington headquarters as a protest against the agency's rollback of net neutrality. Pure genius.
