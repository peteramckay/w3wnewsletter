The latest news about digital independence from around the web:

  • Wired dropped a blockbuster reporting project about Facebook on Monday. The tech magazine's Nicholas Thompson and Fred Vogelstein interviewed dozens of the social network's employees to construct a deeply detailed narrative about a "hellish two years" recently at Facebook. The period has been marred by Russian intelligence abuses of the network, infighting, criticisms by departed executives, and an identity crisis for Facebook that's still playing out. Speaking of which...
  • Unilever is threatening to reduce its ad spending on tech platforms that neglect to reign in abusive content, including Facebook and YouTube. That's no small claim, as the company spends more than $9 billion a year advertising the large roster of consumer products it owns, including Lipton tea, Dove soap, Klondike ice-cream bars, and Jif peanut butter. Going forward, Unilever's marketing chief says it will only invest in platforms "committed to creating a positive impact in society," the Wall Street Journal reports. (Paywall)
  • NPR checked in with several psychologists and other experts about methods to "detox" from obsessive smartphone use.
  • In his latest post for Nieman Lab, Ken Doctor focuses on the brewing "chaos" among West Coast news organizations. Turmoil at the L.A. Times has dominated national headlines, but with much less fanfare, there's also been big trouble lately in San Jose, Portland, and other western cities where revenue-starved incumbents are slashing coverage, Doctor notes.
  • I'm generally opposed to listicles, but this new one from Fast Company is well executed and in service of a good cause: 8 Ways To Make Real Progress On Tech’s Diversity Problem
  • I belatedly caught up recently on this August post by respected web designer Ethan Marcotte about Google's AMP platform for mobile web pages. It's definitely still worth a read. Marcotte, who helped pioneer responsive web design a few years back, says that most of his corporate clients only use AMP because it optimizes their placement in Google search results, not because of the performance improvements Google prefers to publicize.
