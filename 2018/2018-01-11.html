Recent independence-related info from around the web:

  • The U.S. House of Representatives voted Thursday to extend the National Security Agency’s warrantless surveillance program for six years with minimal changes. A bipartisan group of House members pushed to impose significant new protections of Americans' online privacy in the bill, but they ultimately got outvoted. The legislation now moves on to the Senate, which is unlikely to reinstate the privacy proposals, according to the New York Times.
  • An ad-hoc group of developers and content creators has posted an open letter to Google calling for changes to its Accelerated Mobile Pages platform. In its current form, AMP gives Google too much centralized power over the mobile web as a whole, the group says. If you want to sign the letter -- as I have -- you can do so via GitHub. Just insert your name into the HTML for the letter and issue a pull request.
  • Engadget reports that Skype will soon begin implementing an open-source encryption protocol developed by rival Signal to protect users' privacy.
  • Piwik, a New Zealand-based open analytics platform, is changing its name to Matomo. The word means "honesty" in Japanese -- a big priority for the project as it tries to position itself as a more privacy-respecting alternative to Google Analytics.
  • The New Yorker has hired a new director of newsletters -- Buzzfeed's Dan Oshinsky. Nieman Labs reports that this is the first such hire by any Conde Naste publication, prioritizing e-mail as an audience development tool on par with the proprietary social platforms.
