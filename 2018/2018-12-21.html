Happy holidays

Season's greetings! Before we all head off for a well-deserved break this holiday season, I have one last batch of Web 3.0-related headlines to pass along. Or, if you prefer, just think of this as my final pre-Chrismas accounting of who in the tech industry has been naughty or nice:

  • I recently implemented Matomo, an open-source analytics tool, for use on Indizr's website. For more detail, check out my blog post about this decision. It explains in fuller detail why I think Matomo is a better choice than Google Analytics to protect my readers' privacy and why I'd recommend it as something you should at least consider for your own web projects as well.
  • Author David Sax published an op-ed in the New York Times about how society's obsession with innovation can sometimes be taken too far. He writes in part: "True innovation isn’t just some magic carnival of invention, like a Steve Jobs keynote with a pretty toy at the end. It is a continuing process of gradual improvement and assessment that every institution and business experiences in some way. Often that actually means adopting ideas and tools that already exist but make sense in a new context, or even returning to methods that worked in the past. Adapted to the challenges of today, these rearview innovations have proved to be as transformative as novel technologies."
  • ICYMI: The Times also had a big scoop this week on Facebook's ever-widening privacy woes. The company gave previously unreported access to user data to dozens of other big tech companies without the users' permisison. Recipients of this largesse included Microsoft, Spotify, and Netflix.
  • Legendary tech journalist Walt Mossberg -- a major tech influencer if ever there was one -- tweeted this week that he's quitting Facebook and the other social platforms it owns. He will, however, remain active on Twitter.
  • As part of his thoughtful year-end comments on Twitter, product designer and entrepreneur Cody Brown announced he's starting a new group blog and online community called Proxy to discuss "the info war" in 2019. That's his umbrella term for the changing landscape around privacy, user data, and related issues online. Frankly, I'm a little concerned that the phrase "info war" is already closely associated with the batshit crazy right-winger Alex Jones, which doesn't seem to be Brown's intent. But the idea for Proxy as he explains it is otherwise very laudible indeed, so why quibble.

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr today. If you're traveling this holiday season, I wish you a safe trip and good times with family and friends :-)

— Peter A. McKay
