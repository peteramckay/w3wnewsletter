Happy New Year! We've already had a lot of news about online privacy, free speech, and hacking in 2018. Here's the the latest from around the web:

  • DuckDuckGo, the privacy-respecting search engine, reports that its search traffic grew 55 percent to 16 billion total searches in 2017, or 20 million queries a day. That's still a tiny fraction of Google's traffic, which routinely tops 1 trillion searches a year. But it's enough to underscore how DuckDuckGo is carving out a viable niche among consumers worried about their data.
  • Jay Stanley, senior policy analyst for the American Civil Liberties Union, is concerned about the privacy risks posed by the always-on microphones in personal assistant devices like the Amazon Echo. Of course, I heartily agree.
  • My old Wall Street Journal colleague Heidi N. Moore dropped an epic end-of-year Twitter rant about why kissing rich people's butts is a poor strategy for gaining their respect if you're a journalist. The proxmimate catalyst for Heidi's ire was press coverage of the Trump administration. But it strikes me that her message also has a lot of value for Silicon Valley beat reporters as well. I observe that they tend to be rather, um, pliant.
  • Basecamp designer Jonas Downey recently shared in Wired some of the insights and concerns that struck him while doing a year-end gut check. "As software continues to invade our lives in surreptitious ways, the social and ethical implications are increasingly significant. Our work is heavy and it’s getting heavier all the time," Downey writes.
  • Software engineer Michal Charemza posted to his personal site some good tips on writing tests for your code.
  • The Privacy For Everyone conference took place Wednesday in Berlin. If , like me, you couldn't get over there, there was a handy Twitter hashtag that you can still use to view attendees' tweets from the conference, as well as good YouTube coverage of the event.
