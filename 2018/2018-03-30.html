The latest news from around the web about media innovation:

  • Now that Facebook is undergoing a massive privacy crisis, an old web standby -- the open RSS standard -- is getting a second look as an alternative way for users to access news. (We've already publicly been a fan of RSS, for the record. But now other voices are speaking out as well.) Notably, Wired magazine, one of Silicon Valley's bibles, ran a feature on Friday about the need for "an RSS revival." Reader apps Feedly, Inoreader, and The Old Reader get prominent mentions in the piece. And, of course, RSS inventor Dave Winer is continuing his perpetual one-man campaign to keep the open standard on people's radar. He just launched feedBase, a site to aid discovery of feeds.
  • TechRepublic reports that Linux giant Red Hat has now reported an eye-popping 64 straight quarters of revenue growth. Never let it be said that open source can't be good business. The company's secret, says CEO Jim Whitehurst, is to be a highly active contributor in open projects, which thus allows them to shape and maintain expertise in the resulting technology over time.
  • Mic just published a good rebuke of privacy nihlism. That's a fancified term for the unfortunately common belief that users with "nothing to hide" shouldn't worry about surveillance by governments and tech companies.
  • Vox just unveiled a new design template to package related stories together and, hopefully, encourage readers to read multiple links during each site visit. Vox editors told Nieman Labs that such in-house efforts to improve site "stickiness" are crucial at a time when Facebook is tweaking its algorithms to de-emphasize news organizations' posts.
