NYT highlights decades-long trend of concentration across industries

Unless you've been living under a rock, you've probably heard that Apple's share price climbed enough on Thursday to push the company's total valuation above $1 trillion — a first for any U.S.-listed company.

Of all the ink being spilled on that milestone, I just wanted to take a moment to call your attention to a story that's just posted to the New York Times website by my friend and former Wall Street Journal colleague Matt Phillips. (Judging from the website placement, I'm betting this will be on Friday's front page of the print NYT as well. But who knows.)

Matt astutely points out that Apple's run to $1 trillion is emblematic of a broader concentration of U.S. industry that's been going on for decades. The trend is most notably evident in tech, of course, but is by no means limited to that sector.

Among the eye-popping factoids the piece cites to illustrate all the concentraton:

In the past few decades, a profound shift has taken place in the distribution of corporate profits among American companies. In 1975, 109 companies collected half of the profits produced by all publicly traded companies. Today, those winnings are captured by just 30 companies, according to research by Kathleen M. Kahle, a University of Arizona finance professor, and René M. Stulz, an economist at Ohio State University.

Of course, extreme concentration has big implications for smaller businesses, internet users, and consumers, as Matt's NYT piece also points out nicely. Before you hop back on your iPhone to send that next animoji-laden iMessage, I definitely recommend you check this thought-provoking story out.

— Peter A. McKay
