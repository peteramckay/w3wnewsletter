Web 3.0 Weekly

May 24-30, 2020

We urgently need to fix our commons, starting with our understanding of what one is in the first place.

As in many things, Wikipedia is by no means the final word on this question but does provide a pretty good starting point. Here is their definition of a commons, with some added emphasis of my own:

"The commons is the cultural and natural resources accessible to all members of a society, including natural materials such as air, water, and a habitable earth. These resources are held in common, not owned privately."

The internet was also envisioned as a commons, of course. But that vision has gotten quite confused in practice over the last few decades.

We saw that confusion on full display this week after Twitter attached some rather gentle fact checks to a couple of President Trump's ever "reality-challenged" tweets. First there was a thread from him on mail-in voting, then a tweet hinting at violent crackdowns against protestors of police brutality.

To hear Trump himself, Twitter rival Mark Zuckerberg, and various other critics tell it, this was an egregious affront to free speech. In other words, a violation of the commons.

But it really isn't, because Twitter and similar social services like Facebook aren't true commons in the first place since they're owned privately. It doesn't matter how many users are active on these services -- the characteristic that seems to cause the most confusion here -- because the more pertinent criterion is ownership.

If Twitter CEO Jack Dorsey and/or his fellow shareholders want to fact check you in a space that they own, yes, they have a right. And, no, they're not censoring, because they're not restricting your access to the real commons, which is the internet itself.

In other words, you can always start your own damn website, and there's nothing @Jack or any other tech executive can do about it. That's your real digital commons.

To use a metaphor from the physical world, this is the difference between the management of a bar having the right to kick you out for getting too rowdy versus you having the right to walk down the sidewalk outside the bar. One space is a commons, one isn't. See the difference?

Of course, what we often have on the internet is somewhat the opposite: Private spaces, like Facebook most notably, pretending they're the sidewalk itself and abdicating any responsibility whatsoever for checking downright reprehensible behavior in their private space. Then that poison continues to the point that it spills out into the actual commons (the internet itself and sometimes even the offline world) and hurts everyone.

I'd normally share other headlines from the week at this point in the newsletter. But, you know what, not this time. There's really no need.

There's literally a deadly virus spreading through the world, and America's streets are on fire because of figurative viruses of misinformation and hate. The latter category of stuff spreads too easily in part because we grossly misunderstand and mismanage everything I described above about digital commons.

There, you're caught up. There really are no other headlines right now.

Now let's get back to building a better digital commons, either directly if you're an engineer or entrepreneur, or by voting with your feet as a user, which plays a crucial role as well.

Simply refuse to support companies that piss on our shared commons. If that means a little extra inconvenience for you, so be it. (Again, this seems to be a major hurdle for many people, in my experience.) We're all going to need a stronger stomach for a little inconvenience to get through this, frankly.

— Peter A. McKay
