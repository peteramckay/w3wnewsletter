Web 3.0 Week in Review

Feb. 2-8, 2020

Amid the awful coronavirus outbreak in China, we now have a small but noteworthy blockchain use case. This is a good one to illustrate why this new technology really matters.

First, some background: Dr. Li Wenliang, an opthamologist in Wuhan, posted to an online chat room back in late December that he had noticed patients with a new, SARS-like bug. This was the earliest known public alert about what's come to be known as coronavirus.

For his efforts, Dr. Li was "rewarded" with a visit and reprimand from the local authorities for publicizing negative information about China. Because communism. Go figure.

Unfortunately, Dr. Li also contracted coronavirus himself, suffered a long illness, and died from it last week, hailed as a hero among Chinese people grateful for his courage in the face of censorship.

Among the tributes to Dr. Li that have now surfaced is a virtual one created on Friday as a smart contract on the Ethereum blockchain. If you scroll down this page on the Ethereum network scanner Etherscan, you can see the source code of the contract. You'll see, it includes a simple text-based drawing of an "RIP" monument with a message in Chinese commemorating Dr. Li.

Crucially, this little tribute cannot ever be censored precisely because it's stored on-chain, making it immutable. Multiple nodes on the Ethereum network have the information stored, rather than the traditional approach of storing it on a single website's server, or the servers of some centralized service like Facebook. Thus there's no single publishing endpoint that can be shut down or impose its will by taking the tribute off the internet.

Over time, this characteristic of censorship resistance could have big applications in media, the academy, arts, and more. We probably need some additional pieces to help blockchain publishing truly scale, including better user experience and design layers, as happened with the early web in the '90s. (I mean, let's be honest. Etherscan may be useful, but it's not that pretty to look at.)

In the meantime, cases like this Dr. Li tribute provide valuable proof of concept, I think.

On to the week's other major headlines about decentralization:

  • Looks like the long-awaited rollout of Ethereum 2.0 is a bit behind schedule but still likely to rollout in July.
  • The total value of cryptoassets allocated to decentralized finance apps crossed the $1 billion milestone on Friday, according to data from DeFi Pulse. Over 60 percent of that value is tied up in the Maker ecosystem, in which users can use ETH tokens to borrow DAI stablecoins pegged to the U.S. dollar.
  • Steps toward official government-sanctioned cryptocurrencies continue around the world, with other nations clearly nervous about losing competitive advantage to China if it becomes the first mover. Reuters reports that a group of Japanese lawmakers is advocating that their country collaborate with other governments on digital currencies, with an eye toward creating some solution to preserve U.S. dollar dominance of the global economy. The group wants Japan to bring up the topic formally at this yearâ€™s G7 meetings. Meanwhile, the International Monetary Fund, advised a group of Caribbean countries to look into developing their own digital currency as well.
  • The UK technology magazine Verdict reports that aviation companies are forming a new group called the MRO Blockchain Alliance to determine whether blockchain can be used to better track, trace, and record information about aircraft parts and maintenance. Such use cases could save the industry up to $3.5 billion, the group believes.
  • The Wall Street Journal's Paul Vigna reports that cryptocurrency scams took in more than $4 billion in 2019, with the most common type of fraud being old-fashioned Ponzi schemes. (Paywall)
  • CoinDesk reporter Lee Cuen took to Twitter to warn that scammers have been impersonating the news site's staff, asking for bitcoin or offering bogus giveaways. To be clear, real CoinDesk staff (and real journalists in general) do not do this.
  • The Linux-based gaming platform Steam topped 18.8 million concurrent users last week, a new all-time high.
  • As part of its latest quarterly profit report, Alphabet reported YouTube's results as a separate line item for the first time. The video platform had $15 billion in ad revenue in 2019, up 50 percent from the previous year. Just for reference, that figure is getting within reach of the ad revenue for the entire local TV industry in the U.S. It's currently bringing in around $21 billion a year in ad revenue, according to BIA/Kelsey data.
