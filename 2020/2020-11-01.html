In honor of the great Sean Connery's passing yesterday, as well as the fact that one of my quarantined-all-the-time diversions this year has been to watch all the James Bond movies in order, a bit of tech trivia to share:

Connery's second Bond film, From Russia With Love, revolves around 007's efforts to obtain a cipher device called a Lektor that can decode Soviet communications. While the Lektor was fictional, it clearly resembled early real-life encryption devices used in the first half of the twentieth century. The movie goes into some detail explaining what the device is, what it does, and even some encryption basics.

Thus I'm pretty sure that From Russia With Love, released in 1963, contained the first plain-English discussion of encryption anywhere in popular entertainment. Yes, the story is more than a little silly. (Did I mention the Lektor starts out in possession of a hot blonde from whom Bond must entice it? Because of course.) But it's also true that a gazillion whitepapers can't raise as much awareness of new technology among the masses as references like this in a big-budget movie.

So thank you, Bond. A job well done, as ever. And #RIPSeanConnery.
