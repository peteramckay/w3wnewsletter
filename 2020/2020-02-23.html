Web 3.0 Week in Review

Feb. 16-22, 2020

Like a lot of people, I find TED talk videos to be a real hit-or-miss affair. They're either an utter time suck or downright brilliant, rarely anything inbetween.

Squarely in the "brilliant" category is one by technologist Jennifer Zhu Scott on why you should get paid for your data. The talk is from back in December, to be honest, but I just came across it last week thanks to a share from Jonathan Sampson, a developer on the Brave web browser team.

In layman-friendly terms, Scott lays out a great case why and how users are being financially exploited by data-harvesting tech companies. She also touches on the topic of privacy for privacy's sake -- the civil liberties aspect of surveillance capitalism -- but that's not her primary focus.

Much as the civil liberties part is dear to some of us, myself included, it struck me listening to Scott that the "you're being ripped off" frame might be more effective at getting the masses to actually care about Big Tech's practices -- and to act.

Speaking of which: Why are you still using Facebook, again?

On to the latest decentralization news:

  • MIT researchers said they have found security vulnerabilities in the blockchain-based voting app Voatz, which has already been used in Oregon, West Virginia, Washington, and Utah since 2018. The Voatz team scrambled to defend itself, complaining that MIT had studied an old version of their app.
  • New Mexico's attorney general filed a lawsuit against Google for violating schoolchildren's privacy. The company's Chromebooks are the leading brand of laptop in K-12 education, so this is definitely a case to keep an eye on.
  • Big developments at the major crypto exchanges. Forbes ran an in-depth profile of Coinbase co-founder and CEO Brian Armstrong, including some nifty factoids about the exchange giant's growth. Seems Coinbase now has about 35 million user accounts holding some $21 billion of assets, on track to generate about $800 million in revenue for the company this year. On Wednesday, they also announced a new partnership with Visa to offer cryto debit cards outside the U.S. (Still too many regulatory hurdles here, apparently.) Separately, exchange competitor Binance is launching new cloud services that will diversify its business beyond trading, Bloomberg News reported.
  • Things are getting pretty controversial in decentralized finance right now, even by normal crypto nerdfight standards. (Which is saying something.) Traders marveled at a $350,000 profit someone made in seconds using a "flash loan" on the Ethereum blockchain followed by several swaps involving other tokens in a single transaction, all for less than $9 in fees. The trade would perhaps constitute an illegal "round trip" transaction in traditional markets on Wall Street. But on blockchain... who knows? Meanwhile, SpankChain founder Ameen Soleimani said he's found major flaws in the governance of MakerDAO, which he threatened to exploit with a new project mockingly called TakerDAO. Separately, the Depart of Justice indicted an Ohio man for allegedly laundering more than $300 million in bitcoin via a process called “tumbling”.
  • The social network Murmur said it is moving to the Telos blockchain from EOS. Full disclosure: The Telos Foundation, which supports its namesake chain on an open-source basis, is a marketing client of mine. Frankly, I try not to bring them up too often in the newsletter, but this Murmur news seemed pretty significant. For perspective, the TLOS token has a market capitalization around $40 million, whereas EOS is about $4 billion, with a "b." So this is a clear case of an underdog pulling off an upset win. 😉
  • The toll from the coronavirus outbreak began to show in tech. This year's Mobile World Congress, perennially a major conference for the telcom industry, was canceled after multiple sponsors and attendees pulled out due to fears of the bug. Meanwhile, Hong Kong protest leader Joshua Wong predicted in an interview with the Sydney Morning Herald that frustration with the Chinese Communist Party's handling of coronavirus will lead to wider support for political reforms on the mainland.
  • TRON founder Justin Sun took to Medium to recap his recent lunch with legendary investor Warren Buffett, which Sun won in a charity auction. Sounds like Sun gave the "Oracle of Omaha" a real hard sell on crypto and that Buffett repeatedly but silently reminded himself he was doing this s&%# for a good cause.
  • Finally, a couple of notable historic anniveraries, both triumphant and tragic. On Thursday, NASA marked 58 years since the Friendship 7 mission in which John Glenn became the first American to orbit Earth. On Friday, my old Wall Street Journal colleague Asra Nomani noted the 18th anniversary of the murder of fellow reporter Daniel Pearl by al-Qaeda terrorists.

To be honest, I met Danny several times as a young Journal reporter but wasn't nearly as close to him as Asra, who sat vigil with Danny's wife after he was kidnapped in Pakistan. That said, I often think about his loss, which happened just a short time after the 9/11 attacks that those of us based at headquarters in New York experienced from just across the street.

To me, it is all one continuous series of related events. If not for 9/11, Danny wouldn't have been on a reporting trip to Pakiston to report on al-Qaeda in the first place, and thus never would have been kidnapped and killed. That's why whenever someone asks me if anyone from the Journal died on 9/11, my answer is always: "No, but..."
