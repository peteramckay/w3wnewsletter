June 21-27, 2020

Happy to report I've been making some long overdue updates on GitHub that I hope will prove useful to many of you. New stuff includes:

  • A full archive of the newsletter, going back to very early iterations when it was published more sporadically. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y7fc2ryx
  • Additions to my ongoing Web 3.0 for Newbies project, for folks just getting up to speed on decentralization. Link: https://tinyurl.com/ydcjnjxa

Now, on to the latest news:

  • Concern about software-driven mistaken identifications of criminal suspects is growing. Prosecutors in Detroit dropped a case against a man falsely arrested for larceny after the New York Times covered the case, including details of police's use of algorithms that do a poor job recognizing African-American faces. (Link: https://tinyurl.com/y7c82ge5) Previously, Amazon has said it will ban use of its facial recognition software by police for one year. (Link: https://tinyurl.com/y9vkm4jn) Meanwhile, a group of American mathematicians submitted a letter to the American Mathematical Society urging peers to stop working with law enforcement in order to avoid creating "a scientific veneer for racism." (Link: https://tinyurl.com/y9mujbfm)
  • Facebook said it will follow Twitter in putting warning labels on posts by public figures that violate its rules but are deemed "newsworthy" enough to remain on the platform. The move comes amid a growing advertiser boycott under the hashtag #StopHateforProfit. The roster of companies participating has grown to include Coca-Cola, Hershey's, Honda, Unilver, Verizon, Levi's, North Face, REI, Patagonia, Ben & Jerry's, and Magnolia Pictures. (New York Times) Link: https://tinyurl.com/ybj9rd98
  • A timely reminder that individual users can also do your own boycott of Facebook or other Big Tech platforms, if their values don't jibe with yours. The trick to pulling it off, I believe, is a gradual phase-out, or "reverse sunset" approach. Much easier than going cold turkey, especially if you're ditching a certain social network. Link: https://tinyurl.com/ycbnexs8
  • Apple conducted its annual Worldwide Developers Conference virtually for the first time. The giant announced several new product features, including a plan to switch its Mac line of PCs to chips made in-house rather than by Intel. For a fuller blow-by-blow of WWDC, see the liveblog from GeekWire's Taylor Soper at https://tinyurl.com/ydxjpmgy
  • Tom Friedman devoted a longer-than-usual column to assessing where America's economic relationship with China is headed the next few years. (Spoiler alert: Probably downhill.) Link: https://tinyurl.com/y9a6frtw
  • A great new blog post for my friends in media: Consultant Adam Keesling weighed in on why software is usually a better business than content. Link: https://tinyurl.com/yaosm3kl
  • Finally, a tech story with an all-time hilarious newspaper lede from McClatchy's Kate Irby: "A judge has ruled that Rep. Devin Nunes has no right to sue Twitter over statements made by a fake Internet cow, someone parodying his mother and a Republican strategist." Link: https://tinyurl.com/ybvnx4o3

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with the newsletter today! Best wishes for a healthy and productive week ahead.

— Peter A. McKay
