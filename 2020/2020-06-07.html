Web 3.0 Weekly

May 31-June 6, 2020

The link between creating a saner internet and creating a fairer world in general resounded in many of last week's tech-industry headlines:

  • Artificial intelligence researcher Joy Buolamwini warned about possible fallout from use of facial recognition software during the current wave of protests over police brutality. For example, law enforcement agencies may use stored footage captured via body cameras, phones, drones, and other devices to pursue protesters long after the marching has ended. There may also be many false identifications as most facial recognition programs do a poor job recognizing people of color, writes Buolamwini. She's founded an organization called the Algorithmic Justice League to address such problems. Link: https://tinyurl.com/yafbm8zc
  • Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg and Twitter's Jack Dorsey continued to do an uncanny if unintentional impression of Goofus & Gallant in addressing hate and misinformation on their respective platforms. (See CNET's coverage at https://tinyurl.com/y7m5dsot) Meanwhile, Jason Kint, CEO of the trade association Digital Content Next, urged brands to consider the social responsibilities and leverage that come with their $80 billion a year in spending on Facebook ads. Link: https://tinyurl.com/yaavgzoj
  • This seems like a good spot to mention that I had a great conversation last week with Aslen Claymore, founder of Themisia, which is creating a new digital economy for truth. Using blockchain technology, Aslen wants to incentivize and reward people for spreading truth online, rather than the perverse incentives to spread lies on many existing platforms. In that spirit, Aslen is trying to talk to additional folks with journalism experience right now to get feedback to sharpen his ideas and execution. If you fit the bill, please do drop him a note on Twitter @aslenclaymore, or DM me for a more proper intro, if you prefer.
  • Sir Tim Berners-Lee said in a detailed tweetstorm that the COVID-19 crisis has sharply highlighted global inequalties in web access, often along class and gender lines. About half of humanity still can't access the web -- a situation its inventor Lee called "a gross digital divide." Link: https://tinyurl.com/yab9nhpy
  • Billionaire Elon Musk joined the chorus calling for a breakup of Amazon. "Monopolies are wrong," he wrote in reply to a tweet by former New York Times reporter Alex Berenson, whose new book was briefly and controversial blocked from being sold on Amazon. The behemoth later changed that decision following a PR brushfire. (ZDNet) Link: https://tinyurl.com/yb2xxchm
  • Writing for Fast Company, architect Kimberly Dowdell said her colleagues should have an active role in creating cities that are less prone to health crisis and racism in the years ahead. Architecture's multidisciplinary nature, combined with the Hippocratic-like pledge that architects take, are highly relevant to addressing cities' current problems, Dowdell writes. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y83w4fek
  • Finally, if you're too young to know what a Rube Goldberg contraption is, designer Richard Burton has created a handy video to get you caught up. Link: https://tinyurl.com/yb246h8g
