Web 3.0 Week in Review

Jan. 19-25, 2020

In some ways, traditional finance and crypto are coming together nicely right now. In others, they are far apart as ever.

That's the jist I discern from this week's headlines. Take a look and judge for yourself:

  • Vodafone has now left the Libra Association, the latest in a string of high-profile corporate departures from the Facebook-led stablecoin consortium. The telcom giant said it plans to focus on in-house payment products instead. PayPal, Visa, and MasterCard are among the other partners who have also departed the association in recent months, raising questions about the viability of the planned Libra cryptocurrency when it launches. (If ever.)
  • Payments provider Square has won a U.S. patent for a new network that enables seamless crypto-to-fiat transactions. Offhand, it strikes me this could one day be the blockchain equivalent of Amazon's late-90s patent on one-click internet purchases.
  • CoinDesk's Leigh Cuen reports that a new consensus was building at the recent World Economic Forum meeting in Davos: Cash is dead. This and other analysis from CoinDesk's team coverage of Davos are available here.
  • Speaking of WEF, some personal news/disclosure: The forum is pressing ahead to document a toolkit to help supply-chain organizations implement blockchain technology in their operations. This is the culmination of a series of whitepapers WEF has done over the past year, including one I co-authored over the summer. Now I'll be co-editing the toolkit publication as well, I'm happy to report.
  • Venture investors Josh Stark and Evan Van Ness published their Year in Ethereum roundup â€” an annual tradition for Stark and a rotating cast of co-authors the last few years. In the 2019 edition, Stark and Van Ness write: "Ethereum began as a blank canvas... This year, the early strokes laid down on that canvas began to form into a coherent picture."
  • In a new post to his personal site, Union Square Ventures partner Nick Grossman, compares crypto to traditional "bearer" assets on Wall Street. Grossman writes: "By bearer assets, I mean that you just show up with them, and they are respected sight unseen by whatever applications are expecting them." This is a big departure from a world that prioritizes accounts carrying certain permissions, usually associated with some institution, without which it's impossible for individuals to then trade assets. Hmmmm...
  • DigixDAO, a community project that conducted a 2016 initial coin offering pegged to gold, has voted to liquidate its $64 million treasury. Funds will be returned to investors in the form of ethereum, apparently at a tidy profit. CEO Kai Cheng Chng said in a blog post announcing the move that it stemmed from mounting pressure within the community for an option for investors to make a "clean break" when desired.
  • Money manager Peter Schiff took to Twitter Jan. 19 to complain that he'd lost all his bitcoin due to technical issues with an exchange-based wallet he owned, sparking a viral discussion on social. Blockchain.com, which apparently had custody of the wallet on Schiff's behalf, immediately tweeted that they would help. But it's not clear to me at this point from the parties' public comments if Schiff was indeed ever able to recover his bitcoin. Frankly, he mentioned on Twitter that he didn't back up his account's seed phrase â€” usually a key recovery tool. So I'd say his chances aren't good. Either way, the outcome here could become a cautionary tale for other crypto newcomers.
  • Finally, something for the fiction fans out there: Writer Emily Parker just published a short story in Slate envisioning what life in a fully tokenized world would really look like, including some possible downsides.

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr today! Best wishes for a productive week ahead.
