Web 3.0 Week in Review

Jan. 26 to Feb.1, 2020

The biggest social network in a major industrialized country just announced it will launch its own cyrpto token in the network's home market in April, right around the corner.

I'm talking of course about Line, the leading social network in Japan.

What, you thought I was talking about that other network? The one in the U.S.? No, they're tied up right now with regulatory uncertainty, PR headaches, and defections of partners from their blockchain consortium.

This all highlights for me the question of what will be the first crypto product to achieve true mass adoption. It's a real holy grail in the blockchain world right now, something people in the industry speculate about all the time both publicly and privately. Even Deutsche Bank, a sizable player in traditional finance, recently put out a report estimating mainstream adoption will happen in the next two years.

In particular, after Facebook announced its Libra project last year, a lot of blockchain folks figured that would specifically be the currency to break the mass adoption barrier. But now I'd say that hope is fading fast.

For me, it increasingly looks like the next great prospects are in Asia. The region seems to have a freer regulatory environment and -- this is crucial, I think -- a more positive public attitude toward the technology in general. I don't know how the Line announcement in partuclar will work out, to be honest. But let's just say the project that does have me hearing the Jaws theme music in my head every time I read about it is China's plan to launch a national digital currency.

The other realization that struck me recently is that the industry should probably define more clearly what "mass" adoption is, really. China's plan strikes me as the first project that could put a single token in use by hundreds of millions of people at once, which sounds like a pretty good thumbnail definition to me. But one could certainly argue that the bar should be set elsewhere.

Maybe bitcoin, with its $170 billion market cap and who-knows-how-many-users, can already duly claim to have achieved "mass adoption," for instance. Or maybe it just has to double in usage, or increase by some other increment, and we can say the job is done.

Who says the barrier breaker has to be "new" product, after all?

On to the week's other major headlines about decentralization:

  • CoinDesk reports that miners have been hoarding a record amount of ethereum tokens recently, almost $300 million worth at current market prices, in what appears to be a sign of confidence in the project.
  • A report by the research firm Chainanalysis estimates that criminal activity composes about 1.1 percent of global crypto activity annually. (However, as of press time, there's still no word from the Securities and Exchange Commission on what percentage of all Wall Street trading activity is criminal.)
  • Ryan Dahl, creator of the popular server-side JavaScript runtime Node.js, is working on a new security-focused server called Deno, which is nearing a 1.0 release. Given this project's pedigree, it sounds like one to watch.
  • Aside from finally completing Brexit last week, the United Kingdom also announced it will let the Chinese telcom company Huawei participate in the buildout of its 5G mobile network. The U.S., however, still remains hesitant to grant such permission, citing national-security risks.
  • In honor of the recent passing of professor Clayton Christensen, author of the classic business book "Innovators Dilemma," the Harvard Business Review podcast re-released its episode with one of Christensen's talks about "jobs to be done." I love that the esteemed prof, whose work is primarily associated with the tech industry, focused on McDonald's milkshakes in this episode. He truly had vast interests, which is exactly why he'll be so sorely missed.

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr today! Best wishes for a productive week ahead.

— Peter A. McKay
