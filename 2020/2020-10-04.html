#Web3 Weekly

Sept. 27 to Oct. 3, 2020

Crypto exchanges really took it on the chin last week. That should concern everyone in the blockchain space, considering what an important link to everyday users these organizations still are.

Much as we all may love decentralization in its purest form, exchanges provide an important interface layer for newbies, along with those custodial wallets so loathed by OG types. They're important on-ramps in an industry that's generally starved for them.

Here's the latest on the situation, along with the week's other news:

  • Coinbase's CEO said the company must stay neutral on hot-button political and social issues. The new policy prompted a backlash among some staff, which in turn prompted the popular exchange to offer a severance package to anyone who wants to leave because of it. (Bloomberg) Link: https://tinyurl.com/y57bxwxh
  • Federal prosecutors in the U.S. charged BitMEX's founders with failing to prevent money laundering. Separately, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission filed a civil lawsuit to halt BitMEXâ€™s commodity derivatives business in America. (Reuters) Link: https://tinyurl.com/y6y2x6h8
  • KuCoin suffered one of the largest hacks ever at a crypto exchange, affecting over $200 million in assets. The company said it believes it has identified the culprits and is working with authorities to recover users' money. (Cointelegraph) Link: https://tinyurl.com/yxelaayg
  • Ethereum miners made more money from transaction fees than block rewards in September, a first in the history of the platform. Larry Cermak, research director at The Block, broke down the related data on Twitter, though he said he doesn't expect the trend to continue. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y5aewdak
  • The privacy-focused browser Brave now has 19.6 million monthly active users. That figure is up almost twentyfold in less than two years, according to stats shared Thursday by Jonathan Sampson, senior developer at Brave. Link: https://tinyurl.com/yxw4tv7y
  • Filecoin confirmed its mainnet will launch in October. The move is a long-awaited one for the project, which raised an eye-popping $205 million in 2017 to build a decentralized storage alternative to corporate offerings such as Amazon Web Services. (CoinDesk) Link: https://tinyurl.com/yxrbrb6m
  • Smaller rivals accused Google of "rigging" an auction process set up to appease European antitrust regulators. The process, which is organized country by country in the European Union, is supposed to allow competitors gain access to mobile search on Android devices. DuckDuckGo and Qwant were especially vocal in their complaints after mostly losing out to Microsoft-owned Bing in the latest round of the auction. (CNBC) Link: https://tinyurl.com/y6b8e44p
