Web 3.0 Weekly

July 12-18, 2020

As we create the next version of the web, let's make sure it has inclusive decisionmaking and voices from the start, not replicating the current iteration's blind spots.

I'm looking at you in particular, Big Tech companies, with your staggeringly not-diverse workforce. Perhaps if you were to finally address this, your response to hate speech on your platforms would be less tin-eared?

This is all on my mind this week in light of the death of congressman and civil rights hero John Lewis, who was the last living speaker from 1963's March on Washington. (His full remarks from the historic event are viewable online here.)

Lewis's lifelong fight was ultimately one for democracy itself. In my experience, that's a cause most people in tech also care about in their own way. Now is a great time to re-dedicate ourselves to it in full, starting with an internet that is truly for everyone.

On to the latest headlines:

  • The Defiant's Camila Russo reports that the total market capitalization of ERC20 tokens ($33 billion) has topped that of Ethereum ($27 billion), which serves as the technical foundation of the ecosystem. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y5ber9ug
  • Twitter suffered a massive hack in which prominent accounts like @barackobama and @elonmusk encouraged users to send bitcoin to an address controlled by the attacker. The hack, which apparently targeted Twitter's in-house admin controls for the entire network, underscores the need for a truly user-owned internet, writes venture investor Nic Carter. Link: https://tinyurl.com/ycslyb6y
  • The Mozilla Foundation has launched a petition urging Amazon to include its Ring doorbell unit in a recently announced moratorium on providing facial recognition technology to law enforcement. Such software a poor track record identifying people of color -- an increasing concern amid recent controversey over racist policing practices in the U.S. However, Ring continues to share its footage with police through its Neighbors Law Enforcement Portal, which the Electronic Frontier Foundation has called the "perfect storm of privacy threats." Link: https://tinyurl.com/y98acano
  • The Block's Yogita Khatri reports that PayPal revealed in a letter to European regulators in March that the company is working to add crypto trading features, confirming widespread rumors among users and competitors. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y82qatcp
  • The crypto-focused firm Grayscale Investments issued a strong second-quarter report, including client inflows above $900 million. Grayscale says it now has $4 billion in assets under management, including almost $2 billion of new investment in the last 12 months. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y4mf24v2
  • Forbes magazine's Rory Murray profiled Amiti Uttarwar, who is believed to be the first woman to become a core developer for the Bitcoin network. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y9pq7upm
  • Coinbase has scheduled its first-ever investor day for Aug. 14 amid rumors that the crypto exchange is exploring a public stock listing. (CoinDesk) Link: https://tinyurl.com/y7mr72o7
  • A recent enforcement action against Augur may signal that U.S. regulators are increasingly skeptical of decentralized finance tools in general, says crypto entrepreneur Maya Zehavi. Link: https://tinyurl.com/y4sg47h4
  • The Verge's Sarah Jeong weighed in on how social media has adversely affected public discourse. The technology's biggest pitfall hasn't been the spread of left-leaning "cancel culture," but rather a muddying of who's credible and who's not, Jeong says. Link: https://tinyurl.com/ycnp5qzj
  • Finally, some handy astronomy news: Scientists now suspect that if you put a giant black hole at the center of a system, it acts like a parasite, sucking up necessary materials to create new stars. Then the whole system dies. Link: https://tinyurl.com/yyn5vmo6

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with the newsletter today! Best wishes for a healthy and productive week ahead.
