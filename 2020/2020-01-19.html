Web 3.0 Week in Review

Jan. 12-18, 2020

Howdy! I'm happy to report that the newsletter has gotten some new subscribers lately. For me, this underscores how interest in decentralized technology is spreading even among non-geeks right now.

With that in mind, a quick reminder/recommendation to check out "Web 3.0 for Newbies" if you're just beginning to learn about this space. "Newbies" is a collection of links I maintain to help newcomers learn about Web 3.0, including key principles, technical information, and prominent community members to follow on Twitter.

Now let's get to the latest news:

  • A consortium including the ex-chairman of the Commodity Futures Trading Commission and the consulting firm Accenture announced a new partnership called the Digital Dollar Project to promote development of a true national cryptocurrency in the U.S. Crypto users already have several dollar-pegged stablecoins available to them via various community projects and startups. But there has so far been no move to create an official, government-sanctioned crypto USD akin to the national cryptocurrency that China has announced it is working on, possibly for launch later this year.
  • Blockchain is expected to be high on the agenda at the World Economic Forum's annual meeting in Davos starting on Monday, Jan. 20. CoinDesk is running a series of op-eds from blockchain experts about what to expect from the gathering, what its next steps should be, and more.
  • Author and internet activist Cory Doctorow is fed up with the common narrative that Apple is more respectful of privacy than other tech companies. While the it's true Apple's core business isn't targeted internet ads, the company's kowtowing to the Chinese Commmunist Party in its App Store and other sins have produced real harm to users in different ways, Doctorow says.
  • FWIW, a personal note about finding alternatives to the Apple-Google mobile duopoly: I recently ordered a new smartphone, a Moto G7, which I intend to install Lineage OS on. Lineage is a community-maintained flavor of Android that removes the Google-y parts, including the default Google apps that the user can't delete and OS-level data hooks into the company's services. At the same time, existing Android apps from third-party developers will run on Lineage. I've never tried this one before as a daily driver, but it sounds like a good balance. Fingers crossed...
  • As U.S. election season heats up, the role of tech companies in the process is getting increased attention. Slate helpfully reminded readers last week that most computer science experts recommend we don't implement any form of internet voting, as the security risks are too high. Meanwhile, Columbia University journalism professor Emily Bell took to the Guardian to explain in excruciating detail why Facebook’s refusal to fact-check political ads is so reckless.
  • Finally, in honor of the Martin Luther King Jr. Day holiday in the U.S. on Monday, I feel compelled to shout out the great man's Letter From a Birmingham Jail. In it, he defends why he's protesting for immediate civil rights reform rather than waiting for more gradual progress, as some critics at the time advocated. This shows King as doer, not just dreamer, as he's often portrayed in our press. He didn't just have a dream; he acted on it -- an example we can still apply to many things today, I think.

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr today! Best wishes for a productive week ahead.

— Peter A. McKay
