Web 3.0 Weekly

April 5-11, 2020

Week 1 of the global COVID-19 outbreak: "God save us from this crisis."

Week 7 (or 8 or 12 or whatever we're in now depending on how you count it): "God save us from some of the people trying to fix this crisis."

Which brings us to the latest headlines:

  • Google and Apple announced a new partnership to collect geolocation data from up to 3 billion mobile users to track possible exposures to coronavirus. However well-intended, the effort instantly raised privacy concerns, perhaps summarized best by veteran tech journalist Josh Topolsky. He tweeted that the two tech giants are literally making "the phone-tracking shit from the Dark Knight." Sounds about right to me.
  • Writing for Forbes, Kate O'Flaherty gave a nice overview of the benefits that Zoom has enjoyed so far during the COVID-19 crisis (more users), as well as the pitfalls (increased scrutiny of its privacy weaknesses). The company is moving to remedy the latter, but if you're still not convinced, PC Magazine's Michael Muchmore covered several proprietary alternatives to Zoom. Meanwhile, Mashable's Jack Morse highlighted an open-source conferencing option, Jitsi Meet.
  • My old Wall Street Journal colleague Jesse Eisinger fretted that the recent stimulus bills to limit virus-related damage to the U.S. economy are repeating the mistakes of the 2008 bailouts. The legislation so far doesn't adequately target grassroots workers and consumers, according to Jesse, now writing for ProPublica.
  • Speaking of old colleagues, I maintain a Twitter list of WSJ alumni, if you want to give it a follow. Frankly, most of us who covered the 2008 crisis have since left the paper. Thus, if you want to tap that particular bit of institutional memory for perspective regarding the current economic downturn, the Twitter list probably works better than the paper itself at this point.
  • Twitter and Square co-founder Jack Dorsey allocated $1 billion of his fortune to COVID-19 relief. Headline writers for a number of outlets instantly got it wrong by saying he "donated" the money, which isn't technically true as it's not going to a nonprofit. Instead, Dorsey is forming a new LLC company called Start Small, which suggests a possibility he might make money down the line on whatever solutions the effort produces. Or the company could just be a tidy tax write-off as a money loser. Still, it's fair to say the sheer sum of Dorsey's investment is more than any other Silicon Valley A-lister has committed to virus relief at this point.
  • Bitcoin Cash experienced a "halving," or 50 percent reduction in the rate at which new coins are created, without incident. The process raised traders' hopes that the upcoming halving of the main Bitcoin chain will be similarly smooth, according to CoinDesk's Bradley Keoun and Omkar Godbole.
  • A silver lining from the health crisis: NASA satellite data show that nitrogen dioxide levels dropped 30 percent in the northeastern U.S. in March 2020 compared to the year-ago month, due to new restrictions on social gatherings.
  • Unexpected (but hilarious) downside: An Israeli mom went on an epic YouTube rant about the challenges of homeschooling her kids.
