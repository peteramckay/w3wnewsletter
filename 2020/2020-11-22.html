In general, I find there are two big conceptual obstacles that newbies have to clear in understanding blockchain.

The first is the idea of a crypto token being digital but scarce at the same time. In the internet economy as most people have experienced it until now, there's limitless copying of everything, not scarcity. So the introduction of real scarcity is a pretty fundamental pivot to make, with all sorts of implications for everything we do online and any business we might want to build there.

The second is the idea that tokens, being programmable, can be used as more than just a form of money. For example, one could build a playable video game with the right token. If your only frame of reference for blockchain is its parallels to the U.S. dollar or euro, it's hard to appreciate such possibilities for software development. No one ever built a video game that runs its code via government-issued currency, after all.

This is on my mind because of bitcoin's recent run toward a new all-time high -- and thus a fresh round of mainstream news coverage likely to come soon once a new record is set. To the extent bitcoin remains the average person's only reference point to understanding blockchain in general, it strikes me as both a blessing and a curse, really.

On the one hand, bitcoin is an excellent example of scarcity, which in turn is why it works well as money. (That's why I own some myself, full disclosure.) But on the other hand, it's by far not the best software development tool, surpassed in that respect by ether and other utility-focused "altcoins."

So I'd say you're missing out on a lot as a newbie if you don't at least familiarize yourself with the alts as well. The idea of transforming how the internet works through a powerful new development platform is pretty cool, after all. üòâ
