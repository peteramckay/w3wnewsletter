Week in Review: Dec. 1-7, 2019

Howdy! I'll start this time with a happy personal update:

I've started working with a new marketing client, the Telos Foundation, which supports development of the Telos Network on an open-source basis. The Telos platform is a fork of EOS, with some modifications to make it an even more awesome place to build and run blockchain applications.

This is exciting stuff, especially since Telos already has some developers building cool stuff on the network. I'll be working with them to tell that story more widely, along with other big milestones they're planning in 2020.

Now, on to the latest Web 3.0 news from elsewhere:

  • Brave announced solid monthly user growth for its privacy-focused web browser in November. Brave topped 10 million monthly active users for the first time, up 19 percent from October. MAU has also doubled over the last 12 months. Meanwhile, Digital Trends named Brave its "best overal browser for privacy," alongside more qualified praise for Firefox, Tor, and Safari as well.
  • Over 60 percent of the supply of stablecoin on the MakerDAO platform has now been migrated to multi-collateral DAI, according to data published by DeFi Italy. The remainder of the MakerDAO stablecoin supply comprises single-collateral tokens, now known as SAI, backed entirely by deposited ether only. More should be converting to DAI over time as network capacity allows under MakerDAO's recently updated rules.
  • PolkaStats, a network monitor for Polkadot, launched a version 2.0. Spiffy!
  • The Electronic Frontier Foundation has launched a petition drive to stop the recently announced sale of the .org web registry, which is used by many nonprofits, to Ethos Capital, a private-equity firm. The move could lead to higher domain renewal costs for millions of individuals organizations globally, says EFF, which has already collected 15,000 signatures opposing the .org sale. Among them are Greenpeace, the Internet Archive, and the Girl Scouts of America.
  • Google co-founders Larry Page and Sergey Brin announced they would step aside, paving the way for Sundar Pichai to become CEO of Google's parent company, Alphabet, effective immediately. Well, that should free up a lot of the guys' time for trapeze lessons, I guess...
  • Some timely holiday shopping advice from Columbia Law prof Tim Wu: Don't buy loved ones surveillance devices as gifts this year.
  • Thursday was #LoveMyNewspaper Day on social media, a hashtag designed to send feel-good vibes toward beleaguered local publications in particular. Also a good excuse for me to do a Twitter thread about the state of news-focused R&D. Speaking of which...
  • Columbia Journalism Review editor Kyle Pope weighed in on lessons the press can learn from its recent war against disinformation. The fight hasn't been going well so far, in part because the truth alone may not be as powerful as journalists would like to believe, says Kyle. (Who used to be my assigning editor at the Wall Street Journal, by the way. Full disclosure.)

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr today!

— Peter A. McKay
