The latest Web 3.0 news, including Indizr's own content and headlines from elsewhere around the web:

  • The potential good or harm of China's new openness to blockchain continues to be a hot topic in the crypto industry. CoinDesk published a Q&A with an expat Chinese bitcoiner who's skeptical that Communist Party leader Xi Jinping's recent embrace of blockchain may translate into more efficient repression of dissidents. "It terrifies the crap out of me," CoinDesk's source said. "Crypto is technology is just like nuclear fusion is just technology: You can use it to create nuclear generators that might benefit a lot of humanity, or you can use it to create atomic bombs. We do need to think about ethics when it comes to these things."
  • Among mainstream press, the Economist considered whether China's stated aspirations to both participate in free-flowing global commerce and maintain strict order at home are increasingly at odds. Meanwhile, my old Wall Street Journal markets colleague Steve Russolillo has identified the man who helped Xi's learn more about blockchain, leading to his recent positive comments that sent markets soaring. Turns out it's Chen Chun, a 63-year-old computer-science professor and expert at a Chinese government-backed think tank.
  • I've been updating a couple of GitHub projects lately, mostly web design and documentation stuff. First up is Web 3.0 for Newbies, my curated collection of info for beginners interested in learning more about decentralization. It's got a bunch of new whitepapers and suggested Twitter follows to help you get up to speed now. Check it out!
  • On Halloween, Coinbase honored the 11th anniversary of the publication of the bitcoin whitepaper by crunching several fascinating datapoints about the cryptocurrency's growth, as only such a major exchange could. For instance: Sending bitcoin is typically 60x more cost effective and up to 48x faster than an international money transfer, according to Coinbase.  
  • The world of conventional finance also had a major anniversary last week, albeit a much unhappier one: Oct. 29 marked 90 years since Black Tuesday, the Wall Street crash that started the Great Depression.
  • The Bad Crypto podcast caught up with founder Deana Burke, who's building a new app called Gracias that aims to grow bitcoin usage among newbies by using social features and interfaces. Sounds like a really cool idea.
  • Some recent words of wisdom from marketing guy Seth Goldfarb worth re-sharing: "'Blockchain not bitcoin' is a perfectly acceptable opinion to hold and express."
  • It turns out there are tech companies who sometimes get it right on privacy and protecting Western democracy as we know it.  Twitter's move to decline political ads and GitLab's reversal of a decision to increase surveillance are perhaps glimmers of hope that the industry can indeed behave more ethically in the future – if we keep the pressure up as users. Which brings me to...
  • Reminder: To you, it's a smart speaker. To [insert Big Tech hardware maker's name here], it's a smart microphone.
  • Alphabet announced plans to acquire FitBit for $2.1 billion. Bloomberg's Shira Ovide cast the deal as the latest in a series of so-far unsuccessful attempts by Google's parent company to expand into hardware, reflecting deep uncertainty about the future viability of relying on software and services only.
  • Dr. Susan Desmond-Hellmann, Facebook's lead independent director, stepped down from the social giant's board. She cited increasing demands from her day job as CEO of the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, as well as health and family commitments. The opening, which Facebook said it will fill in the next few months, will be key to preserving some level of non-Kool-Aid-consuming accountability for company founder Mark Zuckerberg and his team.
  • Fast Company reports that Vint Cerf, a.k.a. "the father of the Internet," isn't entirely sold on 5G mobile networks as a solution to all of America's connectivity problems.
  • Mutiny erupted at Deadspin after its new private-equity overlords ordered editorial staff to "stick to sports." For many media folks, the episode was reminiscent of Deadspin's old hell-raising sister publication, Gawker. I really miss those guys myself, to be honest.
