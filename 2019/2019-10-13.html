Howdy! A quick recap of posts from the Indizr blog this week, as well as interesting items about decentralization from elsewhere around the web:

  • I've been reading up on Modern Monetary Theory and find this supposedly hot economic breakthrough pretty scary. In particular, it seems utterly out of whack with crypto entrepreneurs' approach to designing new currencies.
  • Participation in the Brave ecosystem is still growing nicely despite recent weakness in the price of its native currency, Basic Attention Token. Perhaps counterintuitively, that's really good news. Here's why.  
  • Also of interest to the media nerds out there: a few thoughts on making digital subscriptions more user-friendly.
  • PR woes aside, let's all remember that Facebook's product just plain sucks.
  • Author Scott Galloway, a vocal early critic of WeWork's now-failed IPO, took to his blog to offer a withering comparison between venture funding for startups and recreational drugs, including remarkably similar self-destructive patterns among users.
  • On Twitter, early-stage tech investor Michael Dearing (@mcgd) struck a similar chord. Referring to the Saudi royal family's investment in We Work, Dearing tweeted: "It’s weird that WeWork hurts Saudi $ reputation in SV worse than when they chopped a person into pieces."
  • Vox reports that new polling shows nearly two-thirds of Americans would support breaking up tech firms by undoing recent mergers, such as Facebook’s acquisition of Instagram. Yowza.
  • Entrepreneur Cheryl Contee authored a post for the Harvard Business Review with this deliciously provocative headline: "Advice on Launching a Tech Startup When You’re Not a White Man." Definitely worth a read if you care about diversity in tech.
  • The NBA kowtowed to Chinese censors, and so did Apple. Guess who caught more smoke for it in the press? (Hint: It's the one with the lower marketing budget.)
  • Looking at the broader picture, New York Times tech columnist Farhad Manjoo writes that Western companies and governments are overdue for a moral reckoning about doing business with China. Their prevailing assumption since the 1970s – that engagement would gradually make China a more liberal society – has drastically failed. Instead, it's our commitment to democracy that's getting corrupted, Manjoo writes.
  • On a happier note, my ex-Wall Street Journal colleague Zach Seward has been appointed CEO of the business news site Quartz. Congrats, Zack!

That's it for now. Thanks for spending some time with Indizr today! Remember, if you're just getting started with cryptocurrency and/or blockchain applications, we've got some cool free offers from affiliates that may be of help.

Cheers,

- Peter A. McKay
