A recap of the latest decentralization news, including posts from the Indizr blog and headlines from elsewhere around the web:

  • The tech world writ large is increasingly wrestling with the ethics of doing business in China, given its constant repression of human rights. Bloomberg correspondent Shelley Banjo tweeted that "a red scare has washed over SF" in the wake of the Communist Party's recent efforts to retaliate for an NBA executive's tweet in support of pro-democracy protesters in Hong Kong. The Wall Street Journal (where both Shelley and I both used to work) reports that the Chinese response has demonstrably included state-sponsored disinformation campaigns targeting Americans on Twitter. In other words, shenanigans very much akin to those by Russia that have garnered so much attention the last few years here in the U.S.
  • TokenSoft's Lawson Baker posted a handy summary of the Ethereum Foundation’s recent DevCon5 conference in Osaka. "It’s pretty clear breakneck innovation can be messy, but Bitcoin, Ethereum, and the greater blockchain industry are starting to grow up," Baker says.
  • The New York Times reports that police databases now feature the faces of nearly half of Americans — most of whom have no idea their images have been stored.
  • The Oxford English Dictionary has now added the term "satoshi," referring to the smallest possible unit of bitcoin trading. Another little milestone for the popularizing of crypto, to be sure.
  • A new paper from the G7 speculates that stablecoins may prevail over bitcoin as a payment tool for the masses.
  • Speaking of stablecoins, he most famous one that doesn't even exist yet – Libra – is continuing to push ahead. The Facebook-led project officially formed a five-member board at a meeting in Geneva. But concerns about the Libra consortium's long-term viability continue to swirl due to regulatory concerns and the recent departures of several major partners, including PayPal, Visa, MasterCard, eBay, and Stripe. Wall Street rival Jamie Dimon, CEO of J.P. Morgan Chase & Co., publicly gloated this week that Libra is a "neat idea" that will never happen.
  • I recently realized, happily, that almost all my favorite tech podcasts are either solo hosted or co-hosted by women.
  • On a related note, blogger Lesa May shouted out her favorite podcasts for UX designers.
  • Finally, a small confession: The blockchain space is both so dynamic and subversive that I sometimes find it hard to tell these days whether a new project idea is serious or not.

Cheers,
