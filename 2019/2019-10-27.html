The latest Web 3.0 news, including Indizr's own content and headlines from elsewhere around the web:

  • Crypto markets soared, with bitcoin enjoying its biggest one-day surge since April on Saturday, after Chinese leader leader Xi Jinping called blockchain "an important breakthrough for independent innovation" in China. His remarks struck me as favorable to an app-centered approach to blockchain – perhaps including tools to enable greater political repression at home. But San Francisco-based VC Dovey Wan makes a good case that Xi actually had currency use cases on the brain – specifically, a digital renminbi that could challenge the U.S. dollar as a global reserve currency. Hmmm...
  • Stablecoins already represent a market worth almost $6 billion, even without Libra. Some context worth keeping in mind amid the relentless focus right now on the Facebook-led project among members of Congress and the mainstream press. There is a lot of innovation in stablecoins going on, and a lot of options available in the market even if Libra never gets to launch, as some folks (myself included tbh) increasingly suspect.
  • Billionaire Marc Benioff and other Facebook critics are now publicly saying the social network should be regulated as a publisher – a classification the company has strenuously tried to avoid over the years. But the shoe definitely fits for a number of reasons, starting with this: Its algorithms are clearly a form of news judgement.
  • Coindesk reports that things are moving fast for the Ethereum 2.0 project, which seems on track for its scheduled in 2020.
  • As promised recently, Brave has begun publishing user growth metrics on its website. The Brave platform, which uses Basic Attention Token to compensate creators and users, has now passed over 300,000 registered publishers. Another promising sign: CryptoSlate reports that Brave's mobile web browser seems particularly popular in Spain, where it is currently ranking 10th in the communications category on Google Play Store.
  • Jack Newcombe, president and chief operating officer of the media syndicate Creators, published a chilling account of his family's Nest camera getting hacked. Using the device's speakers, the hackers berated the Newcombe family's nanny and threatened to steal their baby, he says. Ugh.
  • Runa Sandvik, the New York Times' senior director of information security, tweeted that she's been laid off by the newspaper, which now sees no need for a dedicated infosec person. A perplexing choice IMHO for an organization that's already been targeted by state-sponsored hackers who only seem to be getting more aggressive over time.
  • Ghost announced a 3.0 release of its Node.js-based content management system. The killer feature in Ghost 3.0 is increased functionality for email newsletters, including better monetization tools for publishers.
  • Silicon Valley take note: Nobel-winning economist Robert Shiller says in his new book that the world needs more storytellers and thus – gasp! – more English majors. That will perhaps be counterintuitive to many folks, considering the prevalence of English majors on U.S. college campuses has fallen more than 25 percent since the Great Recession, displaced mostly by STEM. But Shiller notes: “Traditional economic approaches fail to examine the role of public beliefs in major economic events — that is, narrative. Economists can best advance their science by developing and incorporating into it the art of narrative economics.”
  • ICYMI: An important Web 1.0 product – Yahoo Groups – is eliminating all its web-hosted functionality on Monday, including deletion of user data. Here's Yahoo's official explainer, including directions to archive your stuff.
  • Still love this definition of "cryptocurrency" that comedian John Oliver offered viewers awhile back: "Everything you don't understand about money combined with everything you don't understand about computers."
