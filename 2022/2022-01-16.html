Jan. 9-15, 2022

Privacy advocates’ high regard for the encrypted messaging app Signal is almost impossible to overstate. So when one of its co-founders, who blogs under the pseudonym Moxie Marlinspike, weighed in with his early impressions of the web3 movement recently, people definitely paid attention.

Moxie’s post is worth a read in its entirety as he gives an even-handed assessment of what concerns him about web3, what he finds promising, and everything inbetween. Though it all might not be pleasant to hear for hardcore believers in decentralization, this is exactly the sort of clear-eyed critique that can help move web3 ahead, I think.

He also offers two specific recommendations to make sure web3’s promise is indeed realized. Here they are in full, in Moxie’s own words:

  1. We should accept the premise that people will not run their own servers by designing systems that can distribute trust without having to distribute infrastructure. This means architecture that anticipates and accepts the inevitable outcome of relatively centralized client/server relationships, but uses cryptography (rather than infrastructure) to distribute trust. One of the surprising things to me about web3, despite being built on “crypto,” is how little cryptography seems to be involved!
  2. We should try to reduce the burden of building software. At this point, software projects require an enormous amount of human effort. Even relatively simple apps require a group of people to sit in front of a computer for eight hours a day, every day, forever. This wasn’t always the case, and there was a time when 50 people working on a software project wasn’t considered a “small team.” As long as software requires such concerted energy and so much highly specialized human focus, I think it will have the tendency to serve the interests of the people sitting in that room every day rather than what we may consider our broader goals. I think changing our relationship to technology will probably require making software easier to create, but in my lifetime I’ve seen the opposite come to pass. Unfortunately, I think distributed systems have a tendency to exacerbate this trend by making things more complicated and more difficult, not less complicated and less difficult.

On to some headlines from elsewhere:

  • Ethereum’s functionality is growing. The Ethereum Push Notification System went live, enabling the popular application-focused network to offer alerts akin to those users get from traditional websites.
  • Meanwhile, ETH supply continues to shrink. Prince volatility and continued demand for non-fungible tokens (NFTs) and decentralized finance applications pushed the “burn rate” of ether tokens to a new record. More than 18,000 ETH were “burned” on Tuesday, beating the previous high by more than 1,000 tokens, according to The Defiant.
  • NFT platforms both giveth and taketh away on free speech. The Associated Press said it will launch an NFT marketplace on Polygon to help support its journalism. Meanwhile, on a less promising note: NBA ****Top Shot, a popular marketplace for tokenized basketball collectibles, froze the account of a user named FreeHongKong with little explanation. Some fans are worried the incident echoes an earlier one in which the Chinese Communist Party demanded the National Basketball Association fire an executive who tweeted in favor of pro-democracy demonstrators in Hong Kong.
  • In its first week in China’s mobile app stores, the official wallet for the country’s pilot “digital yuan” program was wildly popular. The brisk pace of downloads so far suggests that the new payments system may quickly rival that of WeChat or Alipay, according to the South China Morning Post.
  • El Salvador’s treasury has lost money on its publicly announced bitcoin purchases since recognizing the token as legal tender last year. The government’s return on those trades is about -14%, Bloomberg News reports.
  • The Mozilla Foundation stopped accepting crypto donations, citing concerns over environmental impact. The nonprofit, which makes the Firefox web browser and began accepting bitcoin donations in 2014, said it is reviewing how to proceed.
  • Has Binance founder Changpeng Zhao’s wealth been vastly underestimated? Bloomberg News apparently thinks so. In its initial estimate of the CEO’s personal fortune for its Billionaires Index, the news organization estimated the figure near $100 billion. That estimate, which is based on CZ’s stake in the Binance exchange and its trading volume and valuation relative to rival Coinbase, is at least 50 times greater than previously published calculations by Fortune and others. CZ disputes Bloomberg’s calculation, but declined to provide further specifics.

Bitcoin began the New Year with a few fireworks and a lot of duds.
