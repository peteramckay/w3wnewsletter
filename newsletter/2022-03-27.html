March 20-26, 2022

Since this newsletter moved to Blogchain, I have included a section of highlights each week culled from this platform specifically. For today's edition, picking which posts to amplify in this way was easy, though reading them certainly has not been. Still, I would implore you to join me in doing so.

Of course, I'm speaking of the influx of posts this week by Ukrainians bearing eyewitness to the utterly unprovoked war with Russia that's been ravaging their country. If you've logged on to Blogchain at all lately, you've certainly noticed these voices crying out.

Screenwriter @sazonova has been particularly active, recounting her tearful goodbye to her father as he joined the Ukrainian army and her sad amazement that a full month of the awful conflict has already gone by.

Like many of our new Ukrainian users, @sazonova wrote in her initial post of how radically and instantly her life changed on Feb. 24 when Russia invaded:

"Now, I am a refugee and live far away from my home. I don't know when I will see my family again. My career had been buried even before it actually started. All lost its sense and reason.

"I am lucky enough to be in a safe place and share my experience. At this moment, other Ukrainians are hiding in bomb shelters and struggling to survive. Despite the feeling of total loss and apathy, I decided to use my safety to write about Ukraine. I want to share the experience of living during the war, which now is compering to Second World War."

This being the era of smartphones everywhere, many Ukrainian users are including in their blog posts pictures of destruction and other scenes of war they've seen firsthand.

Dnieper resident @awbdi shared a photo of people camped in one of the area's underground metro stations, using it as a makeshift bomb shelter. @korok from Kyiv shared several images, including one of a bombed-out Russian tank he passed in his car fleeing the city and "before" and "after" shots of a devastated shopping center.

He writes: "All I want is for my family and me to be safe and for this hell to end. I wish everyone that this never happens in your life and the life of your relatives and that there will always be peace in your life."

Some writers also share images and thoughts about life in Ukraine before the war. To be honest, I find this content most powerful of all in some ways because it conveys so poignantly the scope of what's being lost each day the war drags on and the difficulty of restoring it someday.

For example, it is impossible to look at 18-year-old @koshennya's first post, including pictures of her having fun with friends at a nightclub, and not wonder when she'll be able to do that again, to do such normal teenager things.

As its, @koshennya and her family have fled to the Czech Republic by train. She writes that it has been an arduous trip in a number of respects:

"I had a hysterical and nervous breakdown at the station: events that were going around me were so tired that I couldn't go anywhere anymore. I was in a foreign country without proper hygiene and sleep, when every second can be scary in Ukraine and I won't even be anywhere to go back. I tried to calm down, but it was almost impossible. The medical team that took me to my luck station where the good people took us, gave me the sedative pills and supported me."

How Blogchain became a forum for these stories

Capsule Social, which makes Blogchain, recently began offering grants to Ukrainian writers to share their narratives, as well as early access to paid subscription features we have in the works. As our official announcement noted:

We created Blogchain to empower writers. Our platform has been engineered from the beginning as a human rights stack: to make content more censorship resistant, to get writers paid, and to give them more pseudonymity should they want it.

If there is any group of people on Earth who need that "human rights stack" right now, it is people fleeing or speaking out against the war in Ukraine. So it just made sense to get them onboarded to Blockchain.

Reading Ukrainian writers' stories this week -- and emailing directly with some of the grantees -- it also struck me that if this pace of updates continues, it will amount to a pretty extensive oral history of the war, which is important stuff indeed. Even better, it will be a history that can't be erased, no matter how the conflict ends, thanks to our distributed hosting on IPFS and other web3 technologies.

Why write now?

From a creative/editorial standpoint, I also find the sheer perseverance of all these Ukrainian writers humbling. It is amazing that, even under such duress, including life-threatening danger, so many people would pause to document their experience with text and photos to show to others.

This is a profoundly human impulse, I think. By contrast, war is inherently inhumane. So, in a sense, stopping to write and tell stories is exactly part of the medicine that's needed when bombs start to fall. (Or when airplanes are turned into missiles, as I experienced firsthand as a journalist in New York on 9/11, to be honest.)

In his first post about Ukraine, @xaoyo quotes the words that Polish musician Wladyslaw Szpilman wrote about his experience of the outbreak of World War II in 1939. Indeed, his account remarkably echoes the descriptions many Ukrainians are now posting about Feb. 24.

One other observation: While @xaoyo's beautifully eloquent post is written in English, I noticed his bio is in Russian. Out of curiosity to learn more about the new community of writers emerging on Blogchain -- and a marked lack of Russian-language skills myself -- I turned to Google Translate to transcribe the bio.

Here is the English translation it gave me:

"We live in the present, mourn for the past, and wonder about the future. This is the nature of life."
