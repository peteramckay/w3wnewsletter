Jan. 2-8, 2022

 

Bitcoin began the New Year with a few fireworks and a lot of duds.

On Jan. 2, the Bitcoin network’s hashrate, a measure of how quickly new transactions are verified, hit a new record. But then it tanked later in the week after the government of Kazakhstan, an emerging hub for crypto miners, shut down the country’s internet access as protests over fuel prices turned violent.

BTC prices fell more than 10% for the week, extending the token’s year-end swoon.

On a more upbeat note, Goldman Sachs analysts published a new forecast predicting that bitcoin will increasingly compete with gold as a store of financial value in 2022.

Some other notable headlines:

  • Central banks around the world moved closer to offering their own digital currencies. A pilot version of the wallet for China’s digital yuan is now available in the country’s iOS and Android app stores. Mexico unveiled plans for a digital peso by 2024. In the U.S., the Federal Reserve’s Boston branch is looking to hire a product lead for its pilot digital dollar program.
  • El Salvador plans to begin issuing bitcoin-backed government bonds.
  • South Korea’s leading presidential candidate, Lee Jae-myung, is giving away NFTs to his donors. It’s the first time such tokens have been used in political fundraising.
  • Andreessen Horowitz launched a new “web3 policy hub” with recommendations for global leaders who want to turn their countries into innovation centers. Meanwhile, on a less high-minded note, the firm’s co-founder Marc Andreesen engaged in an increasingly hilarious nerdfight with Twitter co-founder Jack Dorsey. Seems Dorsey is a bitcoin maximalist who’s derided the very concept of Web3.
  • For the record, I consider bitcoin to be part of Web3, so we should all be able to get along. But, hey, who am I to get in the way of angry billionaires firing off cat memes at each other?
  • The pro-privacy browser Brave topped 50 million monthly active users.
  • Samsung is adding support for non-fungible tokens (NFTs) to its smart televisions.
  • “No computer network will change the way government works.” That was among the hilariously wrong predictions about the web in this 1995 Newsweek essay I just came across thanks to the Pessimists Archive. They describe themselves as “a project created to jog our collective memories about the hysteria, technophobia and moral panic that often greets new technologies, ideas and trends.”
