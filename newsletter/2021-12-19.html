Dec. 12-18, 2021

Web3 is really having a media “moment” right now. The mainstream tech press, which usually frames every story in terms of a few behemoth Silicon Valley companies, is finally starting to notice the weirdos building smaller, decentralized, open projects that don’t fit the old pattern.

Some examples: Leo Laporte’s widely followed podcast This Week in Tech, which previously didn’t cover Web3 much, has carved out time for it in almost every episode lately. The New York Times’ Kara Swisher devoted her latest column to the decentralization movement. And the business network CNBC ran a story on Web3 just yesterday.

Granted, this wave of coverage hasn’t always been positive. (Witness the take by TechCrunch's Alex Wilhelm on the TWiT podcast recently.) And some of these journalists seem hung up on questions that others of us, ahem, figured out years ago. Stuff like how Web3 is different from earlier versions of the internet and, in some ways, how it represents an attempt to revive familiar good things we've lost over the years.

But, hey, that's OK. Give these people time. They’ll catch up eventually. 

Another small sign of rising interest in Web3, speaking from firsthand experience: Subscribers to this humble newsletter are up 30% since April, and the open rate on each week’s email blast has doubled. So there you go. 

An update to pass along from the ol' day job as well: Capsule Social just launched a spiffy new website, including expanded information for writers interested in the platform we're building, and a form to request an early invite to join. Check it out!  

On to the latest headlines from elsewhere regarding decentralization:

  • Venture invesment in Web3 remains brisk despite a recent pullback in token prices. Axios reports that Katie Haun, a general partner at Andreessen Horowitz who has turned the firm into a major crypto investor, is leaving to start a new investment fund. Meanwhile, Meanwhile, the crypto bank Anchorage raised $350 million in new funding from a group of investors including KKR, Goldman Sachs, BlackRock, and PayPal. Anchorage's total valuation now stands at $3 billion.
  • The venerable auction house Sotheby’s has netted $100 million from sales of non-fungible tokens (NFTs) in 2021.
  • Bitcoin scarcity hit a new milestone, with 90% of the token’s total all-time supply now in circulation. Of the maximum 21 million bitcoins that will ever exist, 18.89 million have been mined.
  • Revenue from crypto scams has risen to nearly $8 billion this year, up 81%, according to a new report from the research Chainalysis. One very notable victim of late: Hackers took over Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s Twitter account last weekend to promote a scam promising free bitcoin.
  • A software bug at CoinMarketCap caused the popular data site to show wildly inflated prices for a number of crypto tokens Tuesday. One ether for $9.2 billion? C’mon now...
  • The International Monetary Fund called for a globally coordinated crypto policy. At the same time, different countries seemed bent on demonstrating why that vision is so unlikely to be realized as they charted wildly different courses on crypto. Russia barred mutual funds from investing in crypto. The Bank of England issued a report warning that crypto could soon pose a threat to global financial stability. In El Salvador, citizens have been wary of a new law recognizing bitcoin as legal tender. But they aren't really holding it against the staunchly pro-bitcoin president, who has an 85% approval rating. And in the U.S., Florida Gov. Ron DeSantis (R) is pushing the state to accept crypto payments in all its official operations. Good luck getting all these people on the same page, IMF.
  • An NFT for thee, I see. The estate of Dr. Seuss has partnered with Dapper Labs to offer collectibles based on the beloved children author's work as non-fungible tokens.
