Oct. 31 to Nov. 6, 2021

I'll stick a little closer to home than usual with this admittedly America-centric update. No disrespect to the rest of the world, which of course has seen a general move toward decentralized tech the last few years. It just seems we had a particularly busy flow of headlines here last week that bears noting:

  • Congress passed a $1.2 trillion infrastructure bill, which includes new crypto taxes and reporting requirements. The measure is expected to be signed into law by President Biden this week.
  • Crypto hiring in the U.S. has been spread widely among different cities. Although major centers like San Francisco and New York certainly have a solid share of workers in the industry, about 53% of crypto hires so far in 2021 have been in smaller chunks around the rest of the country, Cointelegraph reports.
  • The mayors of two major U.S. cities will get paid a portion of their salaries in bitcoin. On Tuesday, Miami's Francis Suarez said he would take his next paycheck entirely in bitcoin. Then New York's newly elected chief Eric Adams one-upped him on Thursday, saying he would accept his next three paychecks in bitcoin. Both have said they would like to turn their cities into blockchain hubs.
  • Non-fungible tokens are taking over New York. So says my old Dow Jones colleague Mike Casey, now chief content officer at CoinDesk, in a new post describing his main takeaways from the recent NFT.NYC conference. The event sold 5,500 tickets, with 3,000 people left on the waitlist, Casey reports.
  • Solana, Lightspeed, and FTX are launching a new $100 million fund to invest in companies "at the intersection of blockchain and gaming."
  • Meanwhile, Jack In the Box is suing FTX over its new "moon man" mascot. The burger chain alleges infringement on its intellectual property because the "moon man" is too similar to its own ball-headed clown mascot, who is of course named Jack.
  • The University of Pennsylvania's Wharton business school said it will accept tuition payments in bitcoin and ether for an executive education course in tokenomics. The crypto payment option is a first in U.S. higher education.
  • More Facebook drama. The social giant said it will shut down a decade-old facial recognition project due to mounting privacy concerns. Separately, the Wall Street Journal reported on a leaked in-house study at Facebook that showed its platform is a compulsion that hurts other areas of life for one in eight users, or about 360 million people.
  • Throwback! On Friday, Prince's official Twitter feed re-shared a 2004 interview of the late singer by Wired. The focus is Prince's early adoption of new technologies, and why he viewed that approach as a way to maintain creative independence. Always relevant stuff. üëç
