Dec. 19-25, 2021

 

Now that Christmas is behind us, it's a fine time both to look back at the year that was and the one to come.

I've decided to do that in two installments of the newsletter, starting with this one summarizing what I think were the top five stories about decentalization in 2021. Next week, we'll take a  more by-the-numbers approach, literally, as we'll have full year-end data in hand by then regarding token markets, venture investment, and other hard metrics about the tech industry.

But for now, let's stick to the "squishier" matter of anecdote. In my view, the following were the biggest headlines and themes of 2021, listed in no particular order:
 

The mainstream is becoming much more aware of the “Web3” vision of decentralized computing.

At the beginning of the year, the terms “blockchain” and “bitcoin” were all but synonymous to most non-nerds. Hence they also thought of the technology only as a platform for building new currencies. But as I’ve noted recently, that’s now started to change in a major way as people realize all sorts of other applications can be built on-chain as well, with profound potential to change how we use the internet outside the influence of traditional tech giants.

The New York Times is now running stories on the topic, usage of the Ethereum-friendly MetaMask wallet is exploding, technical advances are making it easier to host websites on-chain, and more. It of course also helps to get people’s attention that the year’s gains in the token market have been led by altcoins, many of them application-focused projects like Ethereum and Solana.

Bitcoin’s share of global crypto market valuation has now fallen to 40%, compared to 70% at the start of the year, even as overall market cap has exploded into the trillions in dollar terms. In other words, the year’s gains were clearly led by non-currencies.
 

That said, governments and Wall Street have begun taking the potential competition and benefits from digital money much more seriously.

The time for dismissing cryptocurrencies as some wacky oddity that will just go away has now clearly passed. Every major bank, from Goldman Sachs down, is now exploring ways to offer some form of crypto investment to clients.

According to a global survey by the accounting firm Deloitte over the summer, 76% of finance pros believe that digital assets “will serve as a strong alternative to, or outright replacement for, fiat currencies in the next 5–10 years.”

In the U.S., Congress included some new regulations of the crypto industry in its major infrastructure bill. However, members are also clearly curious about ways to encourage growth in the industry as an area of economic leadership.

Other nations’ governments are having similar debates, and many are considering their own national digital currencies, with China taking the early lead in that area. Meanwhile, El Salvador became the first country to recognize bitcoin as legal tender alongside the U.S. dollar.
 

Non-fungible tokens are exploding in popularity.

According to Reuters, NFT sales through the third quarter topped $13 billion through the third quarter, compared to less than a billion in all of 2020. The category has been particularly attractive to athletes and entertainers offering collectibles to their fans.

Traditional art-market powerhouses like Sotheby’s have also experimented with NFTs, as have some media organizations in their search for new business models to support journalism beyond advertising. Meanwhile, the boom has drawn investors who want to build infrastructure to support it, including the likes of Warner Brothers and Microsoft. And there was even a skit parodying NFTs on Saturday Night Live.’

COVID has become a never-ending crisis, with some surprising silver linings for decentralization.

Shockingly, more Americans have died of the virus in 2021 than in 2020 despite increasing availability of vaccines against it. Outbreaks in other parts of the world have continued as well, especially as new variations of the COVID bug emerge.

While that tragic loss of life should never be overlooked, the economic picture has been more of a mixed bag. As remote work has become more prevalent, especially in the tech industry, the traditional pecking order of innovation hubs that heavily concentrate employment and corporate headquarters at the expense of other cities has showed some cracks.

A recent data analysis by LinkedIn and Bloomberg showed that more than half of U.S. crypto hiring has taken place outside the top five metro markets lately. And smaller cities like Austin and Miami have made bold bids to draw companies and tech workers away from California in particular, with some measure of success.

Expect these attempts to continue in the New Year.
 

Facebook's collapse has begun.

Granted, you won’t generally hear the story framed this way by the average tech reporter at a big mainstream news org who needs to maintain access to sources at the company on a reasonably friendly basis. But if you can add two and two, it’s unavoidable that all the adverse developments of late at the House that Zuck Built point to this conclusion.

First, blockbuster revelations by an ex-employee led to a damaging series in the Wall Street Journal and a big 60 Minutes story about how the social giant had neglected user safety for years. Then the whistleblower, Frances Haugen, testified to Congress as well. Then, perhaps most tellingly of all, the company “coincidentally” happened to change its name to Meta, supposedly as a play on the promising new “metaverse” products it’s working on.

Make no mistake, this was a public relations stunt and an attempt at misdirection by a company that’s essentially out of ideas. The “metaverse” is just a repackaging of tech in what used to be called the “virtual reality” or “augmented reality” category that’s been around for years. It has some promise, sure, but it’s not a completely new category by any means, and there’s no guarantee the Company Formerly Known as Facebook will dominate it.

Which is great news if you’d like to see someone else get a chance to innovate, particularly smaller startups that have interesting ideas.

Mind you, I’m not saying that Facebook (current market cap: $933 billion) is just going to disappear entirely. But it could easily stagnate and gradually lose relevance to the point of chugging along forgettably like many, many tech hegemons that were thought unassailable in earlier eras. For instance, even in 2021, you can still go log into your AOL account if you’d like. The service is still there, but no one really cares. That’s what “death” looks like to Facebook at this point.

Whoops... I mean “Meta.”
