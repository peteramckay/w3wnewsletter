Web 3.0 Week in Review

Feb. 9-15, 2020

Howdy! To be honest, I think we had a pretty slow news week this time. But what headlines we did have about decentralization were pretty significant indeed:

  • The Chamber of Digital Commerce, says in a new report that the People's Bank of China has filed more than 80 patents related to its plan to launch an official digital currency. The Chamber also provided English translations of the patents, which suggest the new Chinese currency will be distributed to the public via commercial banks.
  • Meanwhile, fresh evidence of the Chinese government's propensity for surveillance and supression surfaced, lending disturbing context to its crypto plans. Two citizen journalists have been disappeared for publishing information about the coronavirus outbreak, and the U.S. Department of Justice indicted four members of China's military for allegedly orchestrating the infamous hack of the Equifax credit agency in 2017. Almost 150 million Americans' personal information was stolen in the incident. The Chinese government officially denied involvement in the hack after DOJ announced its charges.
  • Hester Peirce, a member of the Securities and Exchange Commission nicknamed "Crypto Mom" for her often sympathetic interest in the industry, offered a plan for the regulator to treat initial coin offerings more lightly. Speaking at the International Blockchain Congress conference in Chicago, Pierce said the SEC should let coins trade for three years before most securities rules kick in -- an approach that would make life easier for early-stage startups. But the proposal isn't likely to get much support from other commissioners, according to Bloomberg News.
  • The Wall Street Journal reports that the Trump administration has acquired access to a commercial database that tracks the movements of millions of cellphones in the U.S. The data, reportedly collected from mobile apps for gaming, weahter, and shopping that ask for location access, is being used for immigration and border enforcement. (Paywall)
  • The Journal's scoop inspired an epic Twitter rant from writer Cory Doctorow about why there's ultimately no point in distinguishing between public or private surveillance of users, as if one side or the other constitutes the "good guys." One type of surveillance inevitably ends up leading to the other, Doctorow says. Noted.
  • The Federal Trade Commission announced it is investigating acquisitions of smaller companies by tech giants Apple, Alphabet, Google, Facebook, and Amazon between 2010 and 2019. Many of the deals were too small to escape heavy regulatory scrutiny when first proposed, but the eagle eyes over at the FTC are beginning to get a distinct impression that acquisitions of startups by Big Tech have perhaps had significant competitive impact on the industry over time. Who knew!
